\begin{abstract}
Modern adaptive routing algorithms react to network congestion through
edge-state information shared across all packets, but they lack
\emph{per-packet history}: two packets sharing the same source and
destination at the same instant traverse the same path under existing stateless load-aware routers (ECMP-style hashing aside),
contributing identically to congestion. We introduce
\emph{mass-aware routing}, a stateful extension to gradient-based
adaptive routing where each packet carries an internal scalar---its
\emph{mass}---that grows monotonically with the congestion of edges it
has traversed. At every routing decision, the packet's mass scales
the cost of candidate edges, naturally biasing more-traveled
packets toward less-congested paths. We instantiate this idea as a
constrained dynamic-programming router (\emph{Mass-Aware DP}) that
computes, for each packet, the path of minimal mass-weighted
cumulative potential within a hop budget, guaranteeing a route
whenever a feasible path exists. On the GÉANT topology, Mass-Aware DP
reduces congestion losses by 60.2\% relative to a Dijkstra baseline
that uses the same edge potential and equivalent warmup conditions
($t = 5.21$, Wilcoxon $p = 1.1\times 10^{-8}$), with negligible
overhead in capacity consumed per delivered packet. The improvement
generalizes across 26 scenarios spanning Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi, scale-free,
small-world, regular lattice, and real network topologies, and
vanishes precisely in uncongested regimes---confirming that the
mass dynamics extracts information unavailable to stateless routers.
The routing decision is taken once per packet at admission, with the mass
scalar evolved internally during the dynamic-programming search over
candidate paths; packets do not reroute mid-flight in the evaluated configuration.
\end{abstract}
